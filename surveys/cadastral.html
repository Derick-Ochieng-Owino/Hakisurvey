What is a Cadastral Survey?

Cadastral surveying is the specialized field of land surveying that focuses on defining, marking, and re-establishing legal property boundaries and registering them with governmental authorities. It is crucial for ownership verification, land transfer, and development planning, ensuring that every parcel of land has a legally recognized identity.

If you are buying property, subdividing land, or resolving a boundary dispute, a certified cadastral survey is the foundational requirement.

Legal Demarcation

Providing legally defensible evidence to resolve disagreements between adjoining landowners.

Creating accurate plans for subdivision, amalgamation, or site development permits.

The Cadastral Survey Process: Step-by-Step

Our certified surveyors follow a rigorous, multi-stage process to ensure every boundary is defined with certified accuracy.

1. Research and Documentation

This is the essential first step where the legal definition of the property is established.

  • Title Search: Reviewing the existing Title Deed, Deed Plan, or Registry Map.
  • Historical Records: Consulting historical survey plans, corner notes, and reference data.
  • Adjoining Property Information: Gathering data on neighboring parcels for alignment.

2. Field Reconnaissance and Monument Search

The surveyor visits the site to connect the legal documentation with the physical reality.

  • Site Inspection: Assessing the general layout and topography.
  • Search for Monuments: Locating existing boundary markers (beacons, pegs, walls).
  • Pre-computation: Calculating approximate coordinates from legal data.

3. Field Measurement and Data Capture

Using high-precision equipment, the surveyor takes new measurements.

  • High-Precision Equipment: Utilizing Total Stations, RTK GPS, or specialized drones.
  • Boundary Determination: Calculating and identifying the exact legal corner points.
  • Witness Marks: Recording the position of permanent natural or artificial features.

4. Boundary Demarcation

Once the legal boundary points are determined, they must be permanently marked.

  • Placement of Beacons: Installing new, durable markers (concrete beacons or iron pipes).
  • Stake-out and Verification: Checking final measurements against required legal dimensions.

A certified surveyor using advanced equipment to capture precise boundary coordinates.

5. Final Documentation and Submission

The final step converts the field work into a legally recognized document.

Plan Production:

Generating the Survey Plan (Deed Plan), a numerical and graphic boundary representation.

Statutory Submission:

The plan is submitted to the National Survey Office for scrutiny and approval by the Surveyor General.

Issuance of Title:

The certified plan is used by the Land Registry to issue or amend the official Title Deed.
