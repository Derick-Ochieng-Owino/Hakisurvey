Ground Penetrating Radar (GPR)

Non-Destructive Subsurface Utility and Feature Mapping

What is GPR Surveying?

Ground Penetrating Radar (GPR) is a geophysical method that uses radar pulses to image the subsurface. It is a non-destructive technique, meaning we can precisely locate buried utilities (pipes, cables), underground storage tanks (USTs), voids, and even geological strata without disturbing the ground.

GPR surveys are vital for damage prevention, risk mitigation, and creating accurate utility maps before any excavation or drilling project begins.

Subsurface Utility Mapping (SUE)

Locating gas lines, water pipes, sewer lines, and communication cables.

Void and Sinkhole Detection

Identifying hidden subsurface cavities or weaknesses beneath infrastructure.

Concrete Inspection

Locating rebar, conduit, and post-tension cables within concrete slabs.

Archaeological Survey

Mapping buried foundations or historical artifacts without digging.

The GPR Process: Radar Imaging and Interpretation

The GPR system works by transmitting electromagnetic energy into the ground and analyzing the reflected signal.

1. Data Acquisition and Field Marking

The GPR antenna is wheeled across the survey area, transmitting ultra-high frequency radio waves.

  • Signal Transmission: Radar waves penetrate the ground and reflect off buried objects or changes in soil composition.
  • Real-Time Interpretation: Experienced technicians identify reflections in real-time, marking the approximate location of utilities directly on the pavement or ground surface.

A GPR unit is towed across the site to generate subsurface imagery.

2. Radargram Analysis and Processing

The reflected signals are recorded as a **Radargram**—a cross-sectional image of the subsurface.

  • Hyperbolas: Buried point sources (like pipes or rocks) appear as characteristic inverted 'U' shapes (hyperbolas) on the radargram.
  • Velocity Calibration: Data processing software is used to precisely determine the wave velocity through the soil, allowing the accurate calculation of object depth.

The radargram image is used to identify the presence and depth of subsurface features.

3. Final Mapping and Reporting

The located utilities are integrated with traditional survey data (Total Station/GNSS) to produce a georeferenced plan.

  • Utility Map Generation: Creating an accurate **Subsurface Utility Engineering (SUE)** map showing horizontal and vertical location of all located utilities.
  • Reporting: Providing detailed reports classifying the quality and confidence level of the located features (e.g., Quality Level B).

A final map integrating GPR data with GPS coordinates for planning.

Key GPR Applications

Damage Prevention

Preventing catastrophic utility strikes (gas, electrical) during excavation and drilling activities, saving time and money.

Infrastructure Rehabilitation

Locating and assessing the condition of aging infrastructure, such as finding voids beneath roadways or tunnels.

Forensic and Environmental Surveys

Mapping contaminant plumes, burial trenches, and identifying unmarked graves for investigative purposes.

Pavement and Roadway Analysis

Measuring pavement thickness, finding sinkholes, and assessing the integrity of roadbed materials.
